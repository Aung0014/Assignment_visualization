What This Tells Us

Malaysia's states are not just different in size—they are economically and demographically distinct nations within a nation.

Summary Findings

🏛 Urban Premium Federal territories (KL, Putrajaya, Labuan) consistently outearn states, reflecting the urban economic advantage.
📊 Scale != Wealth Large states like Selangor and Johor don't necessarily have the highest per-capita incomes. Population scale and wealth are decoupled.
⚠ Inequality Signals Wide mean-median gaps in Labuan, Putrajaya, and KL suggest significant internal income disparities despite high averages.
👴 Aging Challenge States like Perak face aging demographics, while Selangor and Johor maintain younger, more dynamic populations.
For Policymakers: These visualisations highlight the need for state-specific economic strategies. A one-size-fits-all federal policy may fail to address the unique challenges of rural, aging, or high-inequality states. Targeted interventions—such as eldercare infrastructure in Perak or income support in Kelantan—could be more effective than blanket approaches.
